Problems with the DMCA

Every day, the MAFIAA uses the DMCA to take down more “illegal” content, and I for one am sick of this abuse. Some of that content was illegally copied, but not all of it. Not most of it, even. How much of it was covered by fair use? How much was outright censorship? And how long will it be before them come for your content?

Here's how the it works: The MAFIAA uses software “bots” to scan the Web for anything they don't like. Then, they indiscriminatly send takedown notices to everything that even looks at them the wrong way. These bots are dead stupid. So stupid the MAFIAA has been know to mistakenly take down their own content on occasion. So stupid they'll take down content even if they don't own the rights to anything in it.

Part of the idea of the DMCA was that hosts would reject bad noticecs like these, but that isn't what happens. Can you blame Google for not reading the millions of takedown notices they get every week?

Sometimes they will even cut deals with hosts to lay off the notices if the hosts does their own scanning, by even stricter standards than what the DMCA mandates. These “DMCA+” systems are deny users even the limited rights they have under standard copyright law.

The most disturbing incidents, though, are the ones that aren't automated. Because no one reads these notices, the MAFIAA can use them as precision tactical censorship missiles. Even smaller buisnesses are getting in on the action. See how a game studio tried to take down (mentioned at 5:50 in that video) negative reviews of their terrible game.

To fix this problem, we must punish rightsholders when they abuse the system by sending millions of notices. In some countries, it's legal to charge fees for processing takedown notices. This does raise the valid point that this is equivalent to paying sites for hosting infringing content. This is a legitiamate problem, but one that many countries "solved" by having the fees sent to the host's ISP. This practice is ridiculous. The ISP should be no more responsible for the host's content than their electric company. (By extension, ISP's should not be required to censor copyrighted content, as I've seen them do in the UK).

Instead, we should allow fees to be charged only for blatantly false notices, such as some of the ones mentioned in the links above. This will cause rightsholders to stop using robots to send notices (or at lest, to make their robots a little less stupid), and hosts for read the notices they recieve. If Google could make $30 for spending 15 minutes to read a notice, you can bet they'd look at every last one! I don't think this will be a problem in courts—it's obvious when a company takes down a site that has nothing to do with their content.
