My Opinion on Monopoly

Monopoly is a pretty good game. It's not the best game ever made, but it's certainly not the hours-long slog that most people play. It's a ruthless game of carefully tricking your opponents into giving you what you want while thinking they're getting a good deal, then either watching them fall apart one by one, or desperately trying to hold your game together even as the vultures circle and pick out their share of the meat.

It was based on a game made by who? in year? designed to teach its players about the evils of capitalism. In her game, you would first play in a ruthless zero-sum winner-takes-all mode reminiscent of early American business (Need workers with small hands? Hire children!), then the rules would change to those of a socialist ideal, and the players could witness the result first-hand.

Sadly, the sheet of rules included in every game never seems to get read. Instead, people modify the rules in numerous and often counterproductive ways, partly out of ignorance, partly out of a misguided need to make the game simpler. Here, I list a few of them, and their effect on the game.

No Trades

Without trades, you lose a whole dimension of gameplay. Why people would want to consign themselves to the hours long dice-rolling exercise that is Monopoly without trades is beyond me. In several cases, people were surprised when I told them trading was legal in Monopoly. Even after I showed them the section in the rules where trading is defined, they remained somewhat incredulous. Then, they take a bunch of bad deals because they didn't negotiate and began to boycott trades under the belief that they would inevitably be a bad deal.

Sometimes trades are a bad deal, but often two players can make a mutually beneficial trade, especially is one of the players is desperate. Note that the printed value of a property dose not necessarily correspond to the value of that property. In some cases, paying $500 for a $200 property is a good move. Similarly, selling Boardwalk ($400) for $300 or $250 can help out if you really need cash now.

Basically, they're the bread and butter of Monopoly. Leaving them out makes no sense.

No Auctions

When a player lands on property but can't pay for it, what happens? Answer: Players, including the one short on funds, bid on it until one of them makes a bid that no other player will match. This might be lower than the printed price, or much higher.

It's common for Monopoly to be played without auctions, but their complexity is not without cause. They provide another way for money to leave player hands, shortening the game (less money == faster bankruptcy), and serve to distribute properties faster, which also shortens the game. In many cases, they can result in interesting strategies: One time, I bid on a property in the hope that another player would match my bid, only to then win the property at a price much higher that what it was worth. This worked, but I still lost the game.

I think most people play without auctions simply because they don't know they exist. Read the rules!

Money on Free Parking

The Taxes were intended to drain money from the game. PLacing their proceeds on Free Parking removes this essential drain and makes the game longer (see above).

The rest was spent in Afghanistan. 'Murica!

You need to land on a property to build on it.

In Monopoly, you can build on a property at any time.

One of the nastier house rules, since it extends the late game out into infinity by guaranteeing that almost no houses will ever be built. Without houses, rent is like a (non-malaria/West Nile) mosquito. Annoying, but not life-threatening. These mosquito rent rates can't make enough of an impact of a player's budget to bankrupt them. This not only prolongs the game, but also lowers its stakes.

You can only build on your turn.

In Monopoly, you can build on a property at any time.

There's no reason to restrict construction to only your turn.

You can't build houses/hotels between the time a player moves their piece and the time they land on your property.

Alice rolls her dice. She gets a 10. Eve can tell Alice is about to land on one of her properties, so she quickly lays a spread of houses on the color group. Now Alice is out $1,000 more than she would have been.

It's pretty crazy to think this is OK, but this is a valid interpretation of the rules. It's almost certainly not intended, though, so this rule is perfectly OK. Whether Alice can actually carry out the exploit depends mostly on the other players. I tried this once, and the result was a full-blown proletariat revolution.


Why do people play Monopoly this way? I think part of it is that the new generation (including me!) learned it when they were too young to really understand the game. Part of the problem is that the game advertises itself as being for children. No child I ever met had the attention span to get through more than half an hour of the game, with made-up rules or not. Compounding the problem, most of them aren't capable of the critical thinking necessary to play it.

News flash to parents: Your kid is not a genius. In fact, your kid is probably pretty stupid. Hell, I was pretty stupid when I was ten. It's not their fault. Their brains haven't finished growing yet. Just keep them off the sugar and McDonald's and they'll turn out just fine. Anecdote: I went to an awful elementary school. The standard for excellence was, "If you're not screaming or throwing things then who cares if you're learning." But I turned out fine. In fact, I'm a fucking genius [citation needed].

The other problem is that those children (i.e. me) grew up thinking they knew how to play Monopoly, and never tried to learn for ourselves. That's not ideal. I think the only reason some people still try to play it is because they were kids when they first picked up the game. When you're a kid, everything is fun. You can tell by playing and game, especially video games, made for children. If the game doesn't suck ass, then it was probably made with adults in mind.

(Extra Credits did a video on the dysfunction that is kids games, you might want to check that out.)

All this is pretty sad, because now I can't really play Monopoly. Playing a game with a bunch of players who are essentially learning it anew is only rarely fun, especially if they don't take the game seriously. Maybe someday I'll find a group of people who also know how to play, but that will require handling some problems I have. Until then, I'll just have to find other games.
